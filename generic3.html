The Essay: Components of the T-shaped Student

The vertical and horizontal components of the VT-shaped student,
and my experiences fulfilling them.

I consider myself to be a VT-shaped student in progress because of both the experiences I have had and the experiences I have yet to have.

The T-Shaped Student

The concept of the T-shaped student provides the basis for the idea upon which the concept of the VT-shaped student was built. The T-shaped student is the particular model that various leaders in academia and the professional world have encouraged students and prospective employees to fit. The model holds that the T-shaped student has "deep knowledge of a discipline and/or way of thinking, often seen in higher education as a student's major. This is complemented by breadth in a variety of disciplines and ways of thinking coupled with crosscutting skills such as communication, project management, and critical thinking" ("VT-shaped Student").

The Vertical Component of the T-Shaped Student.

The vertical component of my T-shaped student experience includes classes taken within my major - English - or that meet the basic requirements of my major. This includes classes like Critical Reading, Critical Writing, American Literary History, British Literary History, and so forth. This subject area is what I love, is what I prefer to study. I love learning new things, reading new things, and writing about it all. I would say I have met the requirements for this component of the VT-shaped student concept exceptionally well.

The Horizontal Component of the T-Shaped Student.

The horizontal component of my T-shaped student experience includes classes taken outside of my major, and that are housed in other departments and disciplines. Though I am not sure whether the requirements have changed entirely since then, I know that when I was a freshman, I was required to take CLEs, or classes that met the requirements for the Curriculum for Liberal Education concept, requirements that all students had to meet regardless of their major. Luckily I was able to complete most of these by receiving early college credit in high school, though I was required to take Pre-Calculus and Calculus my freshman year of college. Since then, the only class I have taken outside of my major includes Intro to Cinema. I certainly could take more classes outside of my literature based major, that might coincide or supplement my work in my major. That is why I would say that I still have some work to do in this particular component of the VT-shaped student concept.
